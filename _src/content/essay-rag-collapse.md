# Enterprise RAG Is Quietly Poisoning Its Own Well

A meeting ends. Supernormal, the AI notetaker that sat in on the call, writes a structured summary and syncs it straight into the team’s Confluence space. No one edits it. No one signs it. A week later someone asks the company’s AI assistant about that project, the assistant searches Confluence, finds the summary, and cites it as a source. The answer is grounded in a document, and the document was written by an AI. Nobody in the loop knows that, because nothing in the document says so.

This is the loop I want to describe. The tools we bought to read a company’s knowledge have become the biggest authors writing into it, and the knowledge base is starting to feed on its own output. I think enterprise retrieval is heading into a private, per-company version of model collapse, and almost none of the standard fixes will catch it, because all of them assume the documents are written by people.

## What RAG assumes, and why the assumption is the whole product

Retrieval-augmented generation, or RAG, is the architecture behind nearly every enterprise AI assistant sold today. The idea is simple. A large language model on its own only knows what it was trained on, and it will confidently make things up when asked about your company. So instead of trusting the model’s memory, you point it at your own documents. When someone asks a question, the system first retrieves the most relevant passages from your SharePoint, Confluence, Slack, or Notion, then hands those passages to the model and asks it to answer using only what was retrieved. Retrieval works by converting every document into an embedding, a numerical fingerprint of its meaning, and pulling back the ones whose fingerprints sit closest to the question.

The word vendors use for this is grounding. The answer is grounded because it traces back to a real document you can open. Grounding is the entire pitch. It is how RAG claims to reduce hallucination, one industry write-up puts the reduction at “70-90%,” and it is why regulated buyers accept these systems at all: every answer comes with a citation, so there is an audit trail back to a source.

Notice the quiet premise underneath. Grounding only means anything if the thing you land on is trustworthy. RAG treats the corpus as ground truth: a body of documents written by employees who knew what they were talking about, reviewed by other employees, accumulated over years. The citation is only reassuring because a human wrote the cited page. That premise was safe for as long as people were the only ones writing into the store. It is no longer safe.

## The tools are now writing back into the store, and leaving no fingerprints

Over the last year the same companies selling retrieval have shipped tools that generate content and deposit it directly into the retrieved corpus. Not a separate AI scratchpad. The same SharePoint, the same Confluence, the same Notion that RAG reads from.

Supernormal joins Google Meet, Zoom, and Teams calls, generates structured notes from a template, and syncs the output to Notion, Slack, and Confluence. Fireflies does the same, saving both a summary and the full transcript into Notion or Confluence automatically after a meeting ends. Atlassian’s Rovo generates whole Confluence pages, product requirements documents, project plans, campaign trackers, and takes them, in Atlassian’s own words, “from draft to published content in Confluence.” At its Team ’26 conference in May, Atlassian added a Meeting Insights Reporter agent that writes up missed discussions and action items. Microsoft 365 Copilot auto-generates document summaries for any file saved in SharePoint or OneDrive, and Microsoft’s roadmap targets September 2026 for generating finished documents straight from SharePoint form submissions.

Two things about this output matter. First, most of it carries no label saying an AI wrote it, and no provenance link, meaning no recorded chain back to where the claims came from. A provenance link would tell you this summary was generated by a model from that transcript. Instead the meeting note lands in Confluence looking exactly like a note a person typed. Second, in the meeting-notetaker case there is often no human in the loop at all: the tool joins, writes, and syncs on its own.

Now close the loop. The enterprise assistant retrieves from these stores and cites what it finds. It has no field to distinguish a human-authored page from an AI-authored one, so it cites both the same way, with the same confident source link. When the next question comes in, an AI is retrieving and quoting text an AI wrote and presenting it as grounded, a system citing its own prior output. The corpus that was supposed to be the trustworthy floor under the whole system is now partly written by the system itself.

## This is not the open-web problem, and the closed version is worse

The open-web version of this is already documented. In February 2026 researchers at NAVER published “Retrieval Collapses When AI Pollutes the Web,” presented at the ACM Web Conference. They show that as AI-generated pages flood the public internet, search and RAG systems start retrieving synthetic content citing other synthetic content. In their experiments a corpus that was 67% AI-contaminated produced over 80% contamination in what actually got retrieved and shown, and, in the detail that should worry every enterprise buyer, the system reached what they call a “homogenized yet deceptively healthy state where answer accuracy remains stable despite the reliance on synthetic sources.” Nothing visibly breaks and accuracy holds steady, so monitoring keeps reporting health while the grounding quietly stops meaning what you think it means.

That paper is about the whole internet, adversarial search-engine spam, and the loss of diversity across billions of public pages. The private version inside one company is a different animal, and I think it is a harder one.

A company knowledge base is small and closed. On the open web, a niche question still returns dozens of independent sources, so one AI page is a drop in the pool. Inside a company, a specific internal question might have exactly one relevant document, and increasingly that one document is an auto-generated meeting note. There is no diversity backstop, because there was never much diversity to begin with. There is also no adversary. The open-web failure needs bad actors gaming SEO, the practice of engineering pages to rank higher in search. The private failure needs nobody: it is produced by sanctioned tools doing exactly the job they were bought to do, on a schedule, for every meeting. And the retrieval and the generation share one store, so it is a literal closed loop rather than a general worry about synthetic content being “out there.” The buyer’s own pitch, that RAG is grounded in *your* trusted documents, is what guarantees the contaminated documents get treated as trustworthy.

## The honest objection: this is not really model collapse

A careful reader will push back on my term, and should. Model collapse, as defined by Shumailov and coauthors in Nature in 2024, is a training-time statistical result: when you recursively train a model on the output of previous models, the output distribution narrows, the rare cases in the tails disappear, and quality degrades generation over generation. What I am describing touches no model weights and no training run. So I am using the phrase as a disciplined analogy, and I want to be precise about what actually breaks.

The real mechanism is recursive synthetic-content accumulation in the retrieved corpus. Three things degrade grounding. Error amplification: a small inaccuracy in an AI summary gets retrieved, restated in the next AI-written page, and hardens into apparent fact. Self-citation echo chambers: multiple AI documents converge on the same phrasing and cite each other, so agreement across sources, the signal we intuitively trust, stops being evidence of anything. Provenance loss: once the AI text is indistinguishable from human text, there is no way to weight or filter it. The shared shape with model collapse is real, a recursive loop feeding on its own output and losing signal, but the layer is retrieval, not training, and I would rather name that honestly than lean on the drama of the term.

The second objection is that good hygiene contains this. Rovo, after all, still makes a person click to publish a page. Surely human review catches the bad summaries. I do not think it holds, for three reasons. The volume is wrong: an auto-note per meeting across an org is far more than review teams read. The label is missing: a reviewer skimming Confluence cannot tell which pages an AI wrote, so “review the AI content” is not an action anyone can actually take. And the NAVER result is the real problem, the contaminated state looks healthy, accuracy holds, so nothing trips an alarm and nobody goes looking. A single publish click is not review of whether the content is true, and it certainly is not a record of where the content came from.

## Every standard fix assumes documents are human, so none of them see it

Here is why I think this slips past the current playbook. Ask any enterprise AI team how they protect retrieval quality and you get the same four answers: governance, access controls, better chunking, a semantic layer. Governance decides who may see a document. Access controls enforce it. Chunking is how you slice documents into retrievable pieces, and better chunking improves what comes back. A semantic layer maps messy documents to clean business definitions.

Every one of those operates on the assumption that a document’s content is trustworthy and the only question is who reads it, how it is sliced, and what it maps to. None of them ask who, or what, wrote it. A perfectly governed, correctly permissioned, beautifully chunked knowledge base can be 60% AI-generated and every one of these controls will report success. They are answers to human staleness, the old problem of an out-of-date wiki that a person wrote and forgot to update. This is a different problem: fresh, fluent, plausible text that no person ever stood behind.

The defense that actually addresses it is provenance and authorship tracking at ingestion, deciding at index time whether a document was written by a person or a model and carrying that with it. The NAVER authors reach the same place from the open-web side, arguing the fix has to move upstream of retrieval, to ingestion-stage safeguards like “provenance graphs” that flag content with high fluency but low attribution density, text that reads well but traces back to nothing. There is real infrastructure pointed this way. C2PA, the Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity founded in 2021 by Adobe, Microsoft, Intel, the BBC, and others, attaches cryptographically signed metadata recording who and what produced a file, and Microsoft began writing C2PA metadata into Microsoft 365 images in February 2026, partly ahead of the EU AI Act’s machine-readable marking requirement that takes effect in August. But C2PA today is built mostly for images and video, its credentials can be stripped by anyone who wants them gone, and almost none of it reaches the plain text of a Confluence page or a synced meeting note. The authorship signal that retrieval most needs is the one the corpus is not yet recording.

The uncomfortable part is that the contamination is being written right now, by tools the company chose, into the exact store the company trusts most. By the time an enterprise decides authorship matters, several years of unlabeled AI documents will already be indexed, cited, and grounding answers, with nothing on them to say what they are.
