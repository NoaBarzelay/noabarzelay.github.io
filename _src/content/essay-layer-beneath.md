# The Layer Beneath the Agents

In April I mapped the market for coding agent orchestrators, the products that wrap, parallelize, and manage command-line coding agents like Claude Code and Codex. The map covers 45 products scored across 13 dimensions, 585 matrix cells with roughly 92 percent filled from verified public sources. I built it with eight research agents run blind in parallel, so a company surfacing independently in several outputs earned proportionally higher confidence. Four companies surfaced in all eight: Cursor, Cognition, OpenHands, and Conductor. Everything below is a snapshot dated April 28, 2026, and this category moves in weeks, so treat every claim as carrying a 90-day half-life.

I went in expecting one fragmented early market. I found two markets stacked on top of each other, and one price signal that reframed the whole exercise.

## Two markets wearing one name

The wallet here is one wallet. Teams pay for coding agents and for the products that wrap them out of the same budget, which is why my universe counts both, and why an agent like Claude Code and a wrapper like Cursor compete for the same dollar. At the top of that wallet sits an oligopoly of three products: Claude Code at a $2.5B run-rate, Cursor at $2B ARR, and GitHub Copilot at an estimated $1B plus on a reported base of 15 million users. Together they account for roughly three quarters of the revenue in my bottom-up roll-up, disclosed and estimated combined. Below them a commons of more than 30 challengers splits the rest, which is why my model of the non-incumbent opportunity comes out small: $0.8-1.5B in 2026, $2-4B by 2031 in the base case, a shape that produces six to ten sub-billion outcomes and a couple of billion-dollar exits rather than a new giant.

The overall wallet, meanwhile, is bigger than the published numbers say. The analyst TAMs cluster at $9-10B for 2026, but they lock on data that predates Cursor’s sprint from $0.5B to $2B and Claude Code’s from $1B to $2.5B, both of which happened inside two quarters. My roll-up of disclosed and estimated vendor ARR already lands at $7.6B mid-case as a 2026 run-rate, which means the bottom-up present nearly equals the analysts’ forward forecast, and forecasts that arithmetically stale get revised, in this case I expect by 50 to 100 percent. So the market is simultaneously larger than believed and nearly closed to new entrants, and “is the market big enough” becomes the wrong question. The right question is where in the stack anything durable can still be built.

The price signal that anchors my answer is the Windsurf carve-up. In July 2025 Google paid $2.4B for Windsurf’s founding team and a non-exclusive technology license, and within days Cognition bought what remained, product and $82M of ARR included, for undisclosed terms that reporting placed at a small fraction of the Google figure. One company, two transactions in the same month, and the people commanded a multiple of what the product did. The market was saying, in cash, that the orchestrator product is near-fungible and the value sits somewhere else.

## The protocols are the moat-killers

The consensus reading of 2025’s protocol wave is bullish for agent startups: MCP standardized tool access, ACP standardized the editor-to-agent transport, and AGENTS.md made agent configuration portable across products. My reading is the opposite. Those standards were designed to make agents portable, they succeeded, and portability is just the polite word for zero switching costs. Nothing in the stack now stops a team from moving from Conductor to Emdash to Crystal in an afternoon with its configuration intact.

The feature matrix shows what that did to differentiation. Six of my thirteen scored dimensions collapsed into table stakes within about a year: MCP support, bring-your-own API key, worktree-per-session isolation, PR creation, AGENTS.md configuration, slash commands. The complaint corpus I collected from public developer threads converged independently on the same six as baseline expectations, two separate methods arriving at the same answer. The differentiators of 2025 became the commodity floor of 2026. Convergent evolution makes the point from the product side: Emdash, Conductor, Crystal, Sculptor, and Claude Squad all arrived at the identical parallel-agents-on-git-worktrees UX within about six months, and the same core feature now exists in over 60 open-source implementations. Funding across the universe runs from unfunded side projects to $232M, mapped onto near-identical products.

Capital and traction run inversely, which is the strangest pattern in the data. Imbue raised $232M, with NVIDIA and Eric Schmidt on the cap table, and its orchestrator Sculptor has 147 GitHub stars and no commits since November 2025, about $1.6M of funding per star. Conductor raised a $22M Series A with no public repo at all, while its unfunded near-twin Superset holds 10,098 stars and a #1 Product Hunt day. The traction leaders are indies: cmux added 15,710 stars in three months, Steve Yegge’s gastown added 14,751 in four and a half, and opencode sits at 151,000, more than any funded orchestrator in my universe. And almost no orchestrator startup appears on any public benchmark, which is itself the tell. If your wrapper added agentic capability, you would benchmark it, and the absence quantifies how much of the work the substrate model is doing. Devin is the clearest case, reportedly in talks at roughly $25B while Cognition’s own published SWE-bench Verified score for Devin 2.0 is 45.8 percent, against the 80 plus that frontier-model agents post on the same benchmark.

## The top is closing, and it manufactures the middle

Claude Code went from zero to a $2.5B run-rate in under twelve months, revenue drawn from the same wallet every startup in this category is chasing. Codex reached over 4 million weekly developers as a free tab inside ChatGPT plans. Google gave away Antigravity in public preview, built by the same team it paid $2.4B for. The casualty list inside my snapshot window alone includes Vibe Kanban sunsetting at 24,200 stars and Roo Code shutting down at 3 million installs, products dying with traction numbers that would headline a Series A deck. I model each segment’s window, meaning the months before a lab’s bundled agent replicates the product for free: single-vendor wrapper startups have zero, the best multi-vendor desktop plays have 18-24, and sovereign or air-gapped positioning buys 24 plus.

But the encroachment has a structural boundary. A model lab cannot build the neutral cross-vendor version of this product, because a lab that becomes Switzerland cannibalizes its own model lock-in. Anthropic will not build the control plane that makes it easy to route work to OpenAI, and the reverse holds too. Meanwhile every incumbent is bundling its own agent into a seat customers already pay for. I count eight of these bundles, from Codex inside ChatGPT to Rovo inside Jira to Cortex inside Snowflake credits, and each one adds an agent to the enterprise estate rather than replacing one. The switching narratives I collected confirm that buyers accumulate instead of substituting: developers in my corpus average 2.3 coding tools, the most common “switch” is Cursor plus Claude Code rather than Cursor to Claude Code, and enterprise stacks run four to six orchestrators at once. The oligopoly at the top is manufacturing heterogeneity in the middle.

## The layer beneath

The demand evidence for a control plane over that heterogeneity is already loud, it just isn’t addressed to any vendor. The single most frequent complaint in the customer-voice corpus, appearing in over fifteen independent public threads, is unpredictable token and credit burn, including a team whose $7,000 annual subscription was depleted in a single day. A team running Cursor, Claude Code, Devin, and Copilot simultaneously has no unified bill, no policy plane that can enforce a rule like “Claude Sonnet for tier-2 PRs only,” and no per-engineer ROI view. GitHub’s move to usage-based AI-credit billing on June 1, 2026 converts that complaint from a developer annoyance into a CFO line item.

The enterprise wishlist I compiled from engineering-leadership sources reads as a spec for exactly this product: per-team standardization, per-prompt audit logs, SSO and SCIM without a sales call, VPC deployment, predictable per-seat cost. Only 7 of the 45 products pass all four of my enterprise-readiness criteria, drawn from that wishlist (SSO and SCIM, audit logging, VPC deployment, predictable cost), and every venture-stage local orchestrator that fails one fails the same one, governance. The buyers paying the highest prices confirm it: Reflection sells Asimov at $15-25k per user per year deployed in the customer’s VPC, and Devin wins enterprise deals on VPC, RBAC, and audit, not on the benchmark. Governance is already the thing being bought, it just isn’t yet a neutral product, and it is the one asset in this stack that appreciates as agents churn, because the record outlives whichever agent produced it.

So the thesis: the durable companies in this category get built in the neutral, cross-vendor governance, cost, and audit layer beneath the orchestrators. That layer speaks the open protocols instead of competing with them: it meters cost across every MCP server, enforces policy across every AGENTS.md, and keeps an audit trail across every ACP session, so it inherits the protocols’ distribution rather than being dissolved by it. The first incumbent has already moved there. In April 2026 Augment opened its Context Engine as an MCP server callable by any agent, an explicit pivot from “we are the agent” to “we are the layer beneath all agents.” In my 45-product universe, the cross-vendor control-plane square is empty, and that blank cell is the most valuable output of the map.

## What would change my mind

The strongest counterargument uses my own analogue against me. I compare this market’s structure to DevOps 2015-2020, a platform tier over a fragmented tooling commons, and in my read of that market the platform tier produced the 100x outcomes while tooling returned 1-10x. A governance plane looks like tooling. My response is Datadog: the hyperscalers never became the neutral observability layer for multi-cloud precisely because they weren’t neutral, and the company that was became one of the category’s largest outcomes. The disanalogy I have to own is that coding has distribution choke points the clouds lacked: the editor, the repo, the merge gate.

Which raises the sharpest version of the objection. GitHub owns the merge gate, already runs Copilot multi-model, and is already metering agent activity through AI credits and Actions minutes, so the neutral layer could be a GitHub feature by 2027. I think the same cannibalization logic applies, because GitHub is a Copilot vendor, and an enterprise running Claude Code, Cursor, and Devin will not accept the vendor of a competing agent as the impartial meter and auditor of all of them, any more than AWS became the trusted multi-cloud cost plane, even with Microsoft attempting the adjacent move by extending Arc and Defender into rival clouds. But I hold this one with less confidence than the lab version.

The honest weaknesses are timing and transience. Enterprise procurement for a governance product runs 12-18 months against a window I estimate at 18-24, so a startup here can be structurally right and die waiting. And my own bull case assumes enterprises eventually standardize on two or three orchestrators per org, which shrinks the cross-vendor problem. Three things would falsify the thesis: enterprise agent stacks consolidating toward a single vendor instead of the current four to six, the GitHub scenario above playing out with credible non-Microsoft adoption, or one lab opening a quality gap wide enough that mixed fleets collapse to one model.

Until one of those shows up, the structure looks asymmetric to me. Every path that makes the orchestrator tier bloodier, more bundling, more pricing shocks, more agents per estate, makes the layer beneath more necessary, and while the agents churn, the record of what they ran, what they cost, and what they were allowed to touch is the part that compounds.
